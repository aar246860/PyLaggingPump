Analytical Models Guide

A professional guide to the analytical solutions available in Lagwell Explorer, from the classic Theis model to the advanced Lagging formulation.

Models

    Theis (1935) Solution

    The Theis solution is the foundational analytical model for transient groundwater flow in a confined, homogeneous, isotropic aquifer of infinite extent. It describes the relationship between drawdown, time, and distance from a pumping well.

    $$ s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} W(u) \quad \text{where} \quad u = \frac{r^2 S}{4Tt} $$

    Key Parameters & Assumptions:

    • **T**: Transmissivity (L²/T) - Rate at which water is transmitted through a unit width of the aquifer.
    • **S**: Storage Coefficient (dimensionless) - Volume of water released from storage per unit surface area per unit decline in head.
    • Assumes the aquifer is fully confined, has infinite areal extent, and water is released instantaneously from storage.

    Hantush (1956) Solution for Leaky Aquifers

    The Hantush-Jacob solution modifies the Theis model to account for leakage from an overlying or underlying aquitard. This leakage causes the drawdown curve to flatten at later times as the system approaches a steady-state condition.

    $$ s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} W(u, r/B) \quad \text{where} \quad B = \sqrt{T \cdot c} $$

    Key Parameters & Assumptions:

    • Adds **B**, the Leakage Factor (L), representing the resistance of the aquitard to vertical flow.
    • A large `B` value indicates low leakage, and the solution approaches the Theis model.
    • Assumes the aquitard has a constant head and releases water without storing it.

    Neuman (1972) Solution for Unconfined Aquifers

    The Neuman solution is the standard for unconfined aquifers, capturing the characteristic S-shaped time-drawdown curve. This shape reflects a two-stage process: an initial, rapid elastic response followed by a slower, delayed gravity drainage from the pore spaces.

    $$ s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} W(u_A, u_B, \beta) $$

    Key Parameters & Assumptions:

    • **S**: Elastic Storativity (dimensionless) - Governs the early-time Theis-like response.
    • **Sy**: Specific Yield (dimensionless) - Governs the late-time gravity drainage response. Typically, `Sy >> S`.
    • **b** or **D**: Aquifer Thickness (L) - Influences the transition between the two response phases.
    • The model correctly reproduces the S-curve seen in log-log plots. The Moench (1997) solution is a performant and accurate approximation used in this tool.

    Lagging Model (Lin & Yeh, 2017)

    The Lagging model is an advanced formulation that introduces time-lag terms for both the hydraulic gradient ($\tau_s$) and the flux ($\tau_q$). This allows it to capture complex phenomena like finite propagation speeds and memory effects, often observed in fractured or heterogeneous systems where classical models fail.

    $$ s(t,r) = \mathcal{L}^{-1}\left\{\frac{Q}{2\pi T}\frac{1}{p} K_0\left(r \sqrt{\frac{S}{T}\frac{p(1+p\tau_q)}{1+p\tau_s}}\right)\right\} $$

    Key Parameters & Assumptions:

    • **τq**: Flux phase lag (T) - Represents a delay in the water flux response.
    • **τs**: Gradient phase lag (T) - Represents a delay in the hydraulic gradient response.
    • When `τq` and `τs` approach zero, the Lagging model converges to the Theis solution.
    • Particularly useful for fitting early- to mid-time data that deviates from standard type curves.
